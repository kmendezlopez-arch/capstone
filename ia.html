Pros and Cons of AI

Artificial Intelligence (AI) can learn, reason, perceive the environment, and make decisions. Artificial Intelligence was coined in 1956, primarily driven by John McCarthy. However, in recent years, it has become much more widespread and has numerous environmental disadvantages, but also many benefits.

One of the biggest disadvantages is the massive energy consumption, in which a single AI query can consume up to 10 times more energy than a traditional search, and this demand is projected to triple by 2028. This poses significant sustainability challenges, often necessitating a reliance on fossil fuels. Generating images or videos using AI can require 100 times more energy than text-based queries—an amount sometimes equivalent to charging a smartphone.

Water is another resource that the Earth provides us; however, AI machinery requires massive use of this resource. AI data centers consume massive amounts of freshwater for cooling—up to 4 million gallons per day per facility—to manage the heat generated by AI chips. Between 10 and 15 queries can consume up to 500 ml of water, which poses serious risks at the local level, particularly in arid regions. While water consumption is high, data centers also generate significant tax revenue and invest in local infrastructure, thereby creating a conflict between environmental impact and economic development.

The evolution of AI requires immense computing power, driving a refresh cycle of between 2 and 5 years for GPUs (graphics processing units), CPUs, and specialized chips. To meet AI demands, data centers constantly replace servers, networking equipment, and backup power batteries. According to the "Global E-Waste Monitor", only between 17% and 22% of global electronic waste is formally recycled, meaning that a large portion of this specialized and toxic equipment is managed improperly—often by workers in developing countries.

Regardless, just as there are negative aspects, there are also positive elements that AI offers us. Artificial intelligence is improving environmental monitoring; it is transforming a slow process into a proactive ecosystem capable of performing real-time detection. Also, analyzes massive datasets—including satellite imagery, sensor readings, audio recordings, and drone footage—to monitor ecological health and biodiversity.

Furthermore, it contributes to the analysis of complex climate data by transforming this process through the automated identification of trends, anomalies, and subtle patterns within massive datasets derived from satellites, sensors, and climate models. These technologies—including machine learning and deep learning—surpass the limitations of traditional models by handling nonlinear relationships and enhancing both the speed and accuracy of forecasts for extreme events.

Moreover, regarding disaster prediction, artificial intelligence is programmed to identify patterns and detect or predict the location of natural catastrophes, such as floods, earthquakes, tremors, hurricanes, and others. This facilitates rapid preparedness and the implementation of mitigation measures.

In conclusion, AI helps us detect changes in nature, enabling us to prevent them or protect ourselves from natural disasters. However, it also triggers various large-scale disasters that are not so easy to resolve, as doing so requires drastic changes that some people are not prepared to make.
